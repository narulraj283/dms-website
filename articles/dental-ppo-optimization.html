PPO Optimization for Dental Practices: Negotiate Better Fees and Reduce Write-Offs

By Dental Marketing Society March 5, 2026 10 min read

Many dental practices accept PPO insurance plans without carefully analyzing the financial impact. A poorly negotiated PPO contract can cost your practice $50,000-200,000+ annually in reduced revenue and increased write-offs.

Conversely, practices that actively manage and negotiate PPO agreements can increase profitability significantly. This guide walks you through analyzing PPO plans, negotiating better terms, and making strategic decisions about which plans to keep or drop.

Understanding PPO Basics

A PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) contract sets the fees the insurance company will pay for specific procedures. When you're a "preferred provider," you agree to their fee schedule in exchange for patient referrals.

How it works:

  • Patient has PPO insurance with your practice as "in-network"
  • Patient comes for treatment worth $1,000
  • PPO plan specifies they'll pay $600 for that treatment
  • You either write off the $400 difference (if plan dictates) or collect from patient
  • Result: You receive $600 instead of $1,000

Write-offs (adjustments) are the difference between your actual fee and what insurance pays. High write-offs kill profitability.

Analyze Your Current PPO Plans

First, understand what's actually happening with your insurance contracts. Most practices don't know their true financial impact.

Data to gather:

  • For each PPO plan: Total procedures performed, total fees charged, insurance payments, write-offs, patient out-of-pocket
  • Calculate: Average write-off percentage per plan (write-offs ÷ total fees)
  • Calculate: Average reimbursement rate per plan (insurance pays ÷ total fees)
  • Identify: Which plans have highest write-offs, lowest reimbursement rates

Example analysis:

Delta Dental:
- Total procedures: 200
- Total fees: $50,000
- Insurance reimbursement: $28,000
- Write-offs: $22,000
- Reimbursement rate: 56%
- Patient collected: $8,000
- Actual collected: $36,000 (vs. $50,000 charged)

This tells you Delta is paying 56% of your fees. If you have 200 patients on Delta annually, that's $22,000 in annual write-offs to this one plan.

Rank Your Plans by Profitability

Create a spreadsheet ranking all your PPO plans by reimbursement rate and patient volume.

Priority order:

  • High volume + high reimbursement = Keep and grow
  • High volume + low reimbursement = Negotiate or drop
  • Low volume + high reimbursement = Nice to have
  • Low volume + low reimbursement = Drop immediately

Focus energy on the high-volume, low-reimbursement plans. These are costing you the most.

Understand Your Fee Schedules and UCR

PPO plans use different methods to calculate what they'll pay:

  • Contracted fee schedule: "We pay $X for this procedure, period" (most common)
  • UCR (Usual, Customary, Reasonable): They pay based on regional averages. You set your fee, they pay up to their UCR limit.
  • Percentage of your fee: "We pay 60% of whatever you charge"

Understand which method each plan uses. With UCR plans, you have more control—charge more, get more (up to their limit). With contracted schedules, you're stuck with their fixed fees.

Negotiate Higher Fees

PPO contracts are often negotiable, especially if you have leverage (patient volume, new practice location, underserved area).

Negotiation strategy:

  • Document your position: "We're a 15-year established practice with 5,000+ active patients. 40% of our patient base has your plan."
  • Highlight value: "We maintain <2% complaint rate, low claim denials, fast payment processing"
  • Show market data: "Regional average reimbursement for cleaning is $85. You're paying $72."
  • Request specific increases: "We propose increasing cleaning fee to $82, crown to $950, implant to $2,200"
  • Offer volume commitment: "If you increase fees to competitive rates, we'll maintain network status for 3 more years"

Expect pushback. Start by asking for 15-20% increase, settle for 5-10%. Even 5% increase on high-volume plans adds $10,000-30,000 annually.

Drop Unprofitable Plans

Not every patient is valuable. If a plan reimburses at 40% and has low patient volume, dropping it might increase profitability.

When to drop a plan:

  • Reimbursement rate below 50% after negotiations
  • Plan represents <5% of your patient base
  • High administrative burden (frequent denials, slow payment, difficult claims)
  • Dropped you from preferred network (time to move on)

Dropping strategy:

  • Give 30-60 day notice to the plan
  • Communicate directly to affected patients: "We're leaving this plan effective [date]. Here's what to expect. We'll help transition care."
  • Offer "one-time courtesy adjustment" for existing treatment to ease transition
  • Position as: "We can offer better value to you as an out-of-network provider"

Dropping low-reimbursement plans often increases practice profitability by 10-20% because you eliminate high write-offs.

Reduce Write-Offs Strategically

Even with PPO plans, you can reduce write-offs through patient communication and financial policies.

Strategies:

  • Before treatment: Show patients their benefits, out-of-pocket costs. Get insurance verification and patient signature on treatment plan with estimated costs.
  • Use EOB language: "Your insurance allows $X. They'll pay $Y. You'll owe $Z." Clear communication prevents disputes.
  • Collect patient portion upfront: Don't wait for insurance to deny. If patient owes $200 out-of-pocket, collect at appointment.
  • Don't guarantee insurance payment: "Insurance is estimated to pay $800. Any difference is your responsibility." Protects you if insurance denies.
  • Appeal denials: 10-15% of claims can be appealed and overturned. Assign staff to appeal denials, recover lost revenue.

Fee-for-Service Alternative

Consider transitioning some or all of your practice to fee-for-service (no insurance billing).

Benefits:

  • 100% control over fees (no write-offs)
  • Simplified billing and claims
  • Better cash flow (collect from patient, not insurance)
  • Higher profitability (50-100% increase common)

Challenges:

  • Many patients expect insurance billing
  • Requires clear communication and patient education
  • Need affordable payment plans to help patients
  • May lose some patients to insurance-accepting competitors

Some practices go fully fee-for-service and thrive. Others hybrid (accept a few good plans, offer fee-for-service as alternative). Both models work if executed well.

PPO Optimization Action Plan

  • Week 1: Extract PPO plan data from practice management system. Calculate reimbursement rates and write-offs per plan.
  • Week 2: Rank plans by profitability. Identify bottom 20% by reimbursement and volume.
  • Week 3: Review contracts for renewal dates. Identify plans up for renegotiation in next 90 days.
  • Week 4: Begin negotiation with high-volume, low-reimbursement plans. Request fee increases.
  • Month 2: For unprofitable plans with low volume, decide: negotiate or drop.
  • Month 3: Implement changes. Monitor impact on revenue and profitability.

Most practices discover they can increase profitability 5-15% through PPO optimization without losing patients. You're not raising fees on patients—you're just being smarter about insurance contracts.
