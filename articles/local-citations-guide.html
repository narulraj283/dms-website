Building Local Citations: The Definitive Guide for Dental Practices

By Dental Marketing Society March 5, 2026 11 min read

Local citations are one of the most powerful but underutilized tools in dental practice marketing. A citation is simply an online mention of your business name, address, and phone number (NAP) on another website or directory. To Google, citations are like votes of confidence that your practice is legitimate, established, and operating in a specific location.

Practices with more citations and consistent NAP information rank higher in local search results. In fact, studies show that citation consistency can account for 10-20% of your local search ranking. That's significant. Yet many dental practices have outdated information scattered across dozens of directories, confusing Google and losing potential patients.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know about local citations: what they are, why they matter, where to get them, and how to maintain them.

Why Local Citations Matter

Google uses citations for three primary purposes:

1. Business verification: When Google sees your business mentioned consistently across multiple reputable sites, it verifies that your practice actually exists and operates in the location you claim. This is especially important for new practices or those with limited online presence.

2. Local ranking signals: More citations typically means better local rankings. A practice listed in 50 directories will generally rank higher in local search than one listed in only 5 directories (assuming equal quality).

3. NAP consistency checking: Google checks whether your Name, Address, and Phone number match across directories. Inconsistencies create confusion and signal unreliability, hurting your rankings.

Beyond Google's algorithm, citations serve another purpose: they're direct sources of new patient traffic. When someone searches for "dentist near me" on Healthgrades or Yelp, your listing appears. That's a potential patient ready to schedule an appointment.

Understanding NAP (Name, Address, Phone)

NAP consistency is the foundation of effective citations. Your Name, Address, and Phone number must be identical everywhere online. This means:

Business Name: Use your official legal business name everywhere. If your practice is "Smile Dental Group LLC," don't use variations like "Smile Dental," "Smile Dental Group," or "The Smile Dental Group" on different sites. Even small variations confuse Google.

The only exception is if you legitimately do business under a DBA (Doing Business As) name. For example, your legal name might be "Sarah Johnson DDS PC" but you operate as "Portland Dental Care." In that case, your primary listings should use your legal name, with the DBA name as secondary information.

Address: Your address must match exactly everywhere. This includes:

  • Street address format (use "123 Main Street" not "123 Main St.")
  • Suite or unit numbers (use "Suite 200" not "Ste 200")
  • City, state, and ZIP code spelling
  • Don't abbreviate words unless absolutely necessary

Pro tip: Create a "NAP standard" document and keep it in a shared location so your entire team uses the same format. This prevents inconsistencies that hurt your SEO.

Phone Number: Use the same primary phone number everywhere. Don't use a toll-free number in one directory and a local number in another. Stick with one consistent number.

The Top Citation Sources for Dental Practices

Not all citations are created equal. Google weights citations from authoritative, relevant sources more heavily. For dental practices, priority citation sources include:

Tier 1 (Most Important):

  • Google Business Profile: Your GBP listing is your most important citation. Make sure it's perfectly optimized
  • Apple Maps: Apple's business directory is increasingly important, especially for iOS users
  • Bing Places: Microsoft's directory carries significant weight
  • Healthgrades.com: One of the most respected directories for healthcare providers. Patients use this extensively
  • Zocdoc.com: Major appointment booking platform with millions of users searching for dentists

Tier 2 (Highly Important):

  • Yelp: Consumer review site with massive traffic and search ranking authority
  • Better Business Bureau (BBB): Trust signal for older demographics especially
  • Waze: Local business directory often used for navigation and discovery
  • DexKnows: Dental-specific directory with good authority
  • Psychology Today (for therapists/behavioral health, if applicable)

Tier 3 (Important):

  • Local chamber of commerce website
  • Local "best of" directories
  • State dental board directories
  • Local business listing sites
  • Industry-specific directories
  • Professional association websites (ADA, state dental societies)

Focus first on Tier 1 and 2 sources. These carry the most weight with Google and drive the most patient traffic. Tier 3 citations provide supporting evidence that your practice is legitimate, but shouldn't be your primary focus.

Step-by-Step: Building Your Citation Portfolio

Step 1: Claim and Optimize Existing Citations

Before creating new citations, claim and optimize any that already exist. Search Google for your practice name and check what comes up. You might already be listed on directories you didn't know about—and some might have outdated information.

For each directory you find:

  • Check if your information is accurate
  • Claim the listing if you haven't already (most directories require verification)
  • Update any incorrect information immediately
  • Add photos and detailed descriptions if the directory allows it
  • Set up alerts so you're notified if information changes

Step 2: Create Accounts on Tier 1 Directories

Systematically go through each Tier 1 directory and create or claim your listing. For each, you'll typically need to:

  • Enter your practice name, address, and phone number (use your NAP standard)
  • Add your website URL
  • Upload photos (professional exterior, interior, team photos)
  • Write a compelling business description
  • Verify your listing (via email, phone call, or postcard)
  • Publish

This process typically takes 30-60 minutes per directory. For Google Business Profile and Yelp, allow an hour each since they require careful optimization.

Step 3: Submit to Tier 2 Directories

Once you've completed Tier 1, move on to Tier 2 directories. Follow the same process: create listings, verify, optimize, and publish.

Step 4: Add Supporting Tier 3 Citations

After establishing presence in the most important directories, add citations to local and industry-specific directories. These don't require as much optimization effort but provide supporting signals to Google.

Conducting a Citation Audit

If your practice has been around for a while, you probably have citations scattered across the internet—some of which might have outdated information. A citation audit helps you identify and fix these problems.

How to conduct a citation audit:

1. Use a citation audit tool (Moz Local, BrightLocal, SEMrush) to scan the web for mentions of your business name, address, and phone number

2. Export the results and organize them by:

  • Directory name
  • Accuracy of information (correct/incorrect)
  • Authority of the source (high/medium/low)

3. Prioritize fixes by addressing the most authoritative directories first

4. For directories with your business listed:

  • If information is correct: do nothing
  • If information is outdated: update it immediately
  • If address or NAP doesn't match your current listing: fix it

5. For low-authority or spam directories with incorrect information:

  • Try to claim the listing and delete it if possible
  • If you can't delete it, contact the directory owner requesting removal
  • Document the effort (screenshot the request) in case you need it for future disputes

Common citation problems to fix:

  • Old addresses (if you've moved)
  • Disconnected phone numbers
  • Incorrect business name variations
  • Missing or incorrect hours of operation
  • Wrong website URL
  • Duplicate listings (multiple profiles for the same location)

Managing Multiple Locations

If you operate multiple dental offices, you need a separate citation strategy for each location.

Best practices for multi-location practices:

  • Separate NAP for each location: Each office should have its own unique address and phone number
  • Consistent branding: Use the parent practice name with location identifier. Example: "Smile Dental - Downtown Portland" and "Smile Dental - Northeast Portland"
  • Separate GBP profiles: Create individual Google Business Profiles for each location
  • Location-specific citations: Each location should have its own citations with appropriate NAP information
  • Separate website pages: Create dedicated pages for each location on your main website

Do not create one citation with multiple addresses. Google treats this as a single business, which hurts rankings for all your locations.

Managing Your Citations Long-Term

Citation management isn't a one-time project. You need an ongoing system to maintain accuracy and add new citations over time.

Monthly citation checklist:

  • Check Google Business Profile for any updates or changes
  • Monitor your top 5 directories for incorrect information
  • Review new patient calls to understand which directories are driving traffic
  • Respond to reviews on directories where patients leave feedback
  • Look for any new citations that appeared (and verify they're accurate)

Quarterly tasks:

  • Add a new citation to a directory you haven't claimed yet
  • Update photos on your top directories
  • Audit your NAP consistency across all listings
  • Check for duplicate or inaccurate listings

What About Social Media and Local Directories?

Social media profiles (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn) count as citations. Make sure your business information is consistent on these platforms too:

  • Facebook business page with correct address and phone
  • Instagram business profile with linked website
  • LinkedIn company page (if applicable)
  • YouTube channel with practice information

These aren't as important as business directories like Healthgrades or Google, but they should still be consistent with your NAP standard.

The Citation-Rankings Connection

How much impact do citations have on rankings? Research from Local SEO Guide found that citation consistency is correlated with 10-20% of local search ranking factors. This varies by market competitiveness, but it's significant enough to make a real difference.

In a competitive market with 10+ dental practices, the difference between having 5 citations and 50 citations can mean the difference between ranking #1 and ranking #8 in local search results.

More citations also mean more exposure. Even if a citation doesn't directly rank a page in search, it's another place a patient might discover your practice.

Avoiding Citation Mistakes

Don't: Inconsistently format your NAP across directories

This is the #1 citation mistake. "123 Main Street Suite 100" in one directory and "123 Main St, Ste 100" in another creates confusion and ranking penalties.

Don't: Use a toll-free number or Google Voice number as your primary contact

Google wants your actual business phone number. Toll-free numbers can be shared across multiple locations, which confuses the algorithm.

Don't: Ignore low-quality directories

If you're listed on a spam directory with incorrect information, it can hurt you. Monitor your presence and request removal from low-quality sources.

Don't: Neglect local directories in favor of national ones

Healthgrades and Zocdoc are great, but don't ignore your local chamber of commerce or city business directory. Local citations send strong local signals.

Don't: Set and forget

Citations require ongoing management. Set a monthly reminder to audit your key directories and ensure information is current.

Putting It All Together

Building a strong citation portfolio is one of the highest-ROI activities you can do for your dental practice's local SEO. The combination of claimed citations, consistent NAP information, and authoritative directory presence signals legitimacy to Google and directly impacts your rankings.

Start by auditing your existing citations and fixing any inconsistencies. Then systematically claim and optimize listings on the most important directories. Finally, establish a system for ongoing management and monitoring. Done consistently, citations will be a major driver of new patient acquisition for your practice.
