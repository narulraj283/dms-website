How to Respond to Negative Dental Reviews (Templates Included)

By Dental Marketing Society March 5, 2026 9 min read

Every dental practice gets negative reviews. No matter how excellent your care, how kind your staff, or how beautiful your office, someone will post a 1-star review expressing frustration. The difference between practices that turn negative reviews into opportunities and those that watch their reputation spiral is how they respond.

A professional, empathetic response to a negative review actually improves your reputation. Prospective patients see that you care about feedback and are willing to make things right. Ignoring negative reviews, meanwhile, signals indifference and can damage your practice's credibility.

This guide teaches you how to respond to negative reviews professionally, maintain HIPAA compliance, and sometimes even turn an angry patient into a loyal advocate.

Why Responding Matters

Studies show that practices which respond to every review (positive and negative) have:

  • Higher average ratings (because response shows care)
  • Better local search rankings (Google rewards engagement)
  • More trust from prospective patients (seeing accountability)
  • Better patient loyalty (patients feel heard)

One unanswered negative review sends a message: "We don't care about your experience." Five unanswered negative reviews? Your practice looks indifferent. Respond to all of them, and you look responsive and professional.

The Response Framework: HEAR

When responding to negative reviews, use the HEAR framework:

H - Humanize

Start by acknowledging their feelings. Don't dismiss their complaint or go on the defensive. Show empathy.

Example: "We're sorry to hear you had a disappointing experience. We understand how frustrating that must be."

E - Explain

Provide context without making excuses. If there's a misunderstanding, gently clarify. If there's a legitimate issue, acknowledge it.

Example: "We take extensive time with every patient to ensure their comfort. We'd love to understand what happened during your visit."

A - Action

Offer concrete steps to resolve the issue. This shows you're serious about improvement.

Example: "We'd like to discuss this further and make it right. Please call us at [phone] or email [email] so we can resolve this privately."

R - Respect

Thank them for the feedback, even if the review is negative. Show respect for their willingness to share their experience.

Example: "Thank you for bringing this to our attention. We take all feedback seriously and use it to improve our practice."

Response Template: General Negative Review

"Thank you for taking the time to share your feedback. We're genuinely sorry your experience didn't meet your expectations. Patient satisfaction is our top priority, and we would very much like the opportunity to understand what went wrong and make it right.

We'd appreciate the chance to discuss this with you privately. Please contact us at [phone number] or [email address] at your earliest convenience. We're committed to resolving this issue and earning back your trust.

Thank you again for giving us the opportunity to improve."

Specific Scenarios and Responses

Scenario 1: Cost/Price Complaint

"We appreciate your feedback regarding the cost of your treatment. We understand that dental care is a significant investment, and we always strive to provide the best value for your money. We'd love to discuss your concerns about our pricing and explore options that might work better for your situation. Please reach out to us so we can have a conversation about this."

Scenario 2: Staff Rudeness/Unprofessional Behavior

"We sincerely apologize if our staff member's behavior fell short of our standards. We pride ourselves on providing compassionate, professional care, and we're disappointed to hear that wasn't your experience. This type of feedback is important to us, and we take it very seriously. We'd like to speak with you about this privately and discuss how we can improve. Please contact us at [phone]."

Scenario 3: Treatment Outcome Complaint

"Thank you for sharing your concerns about your treatment results. We understand your frustration and want to ensure you're completely satisfied. We'd like to schedule a follow-up consultation to evaluate your treatment and discuss any adjustments or solutions. Please call us at [phone] to arrange a time."

Scenario 4: Wait Time/Scheduling

"We appreciate your feedback about your appointment experience. We're always working to improve our scheduling and reduce wait times for our patients. Your input helps us identify areas for improvement. We'd like to discuss this with you and understand your concerns better. Please reach out to us."

Scenario 5: Pain During Procedure

"We're sorry to hear you experienced discomfort during your procedure. Your comfort is extremely important to us, and we always have options to manage pain. We'd like to discuss what happened and ensure that future visits are as comfortable as possible for you. Please contact us so we can schedule a consultation."

HIPAA Compliance: What You Can and Cannot Say

Healthcare providers must be careful not to disclose patient health information in public reviews. This is regulated by HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act).

What you CAN say:

  • General statements about your treatment philosophy
  • Information about office policies
  • Invitations to discuss the issue privately
  • General statements without identifying information

What you CANNOT say:

  • Specific treatment details or diagnoses
  • "You came in for a root canal" (reveals their condition)
  • "Your X-rays showed..." (reveals clinical findings)
  • Medication names or treatment specifics
  • Appointment dates or visit frequency

Safe response to a complaint about a specific procedure:

"We appreciate your feedback. We'd like to discuss your concerns in a private setting where we can review your specific situation. Please contact us at [phone] to schedule a confidential consultation."

By inviting private discussion, you avoid disclosing health information publicly while still responding professionally.

Response Timing

Respond within 24-48 hours of the review. Quick responses show you're engaged and take feedback seriously. Late responses (after a week) seem less genuine and let the complaint fester.

If you can't respond immediately, check your reviews daily and prioritize responses to negative ones.

What NOT to Do When Responding

Don't be defensive: "That's not what happened" puts readers on the side of the reviewer. Let them tell their story.

Don't argue with the patient: An argument in the comments makes you look bad, regardless of who's right.

Don't question their truthfulness: Even if the review is completely false, saying "this is false" only draws more attention and comes across as defensive.

Don't overshare: Detailed explanations of what "really happened" come across as making excuses. Invite private discussion instead.

Don't ignore repeated issues: If you get multiple complaints about the same thing (wait times, a specific staff member), take it seriously and implement changes.

Turning Negative Reviews into Positives

Sometimes, by responding professionally and resolving the patient's issue, they'll delete the negative review or even post a positive follow-up review. This happens when patients see you actually care.

Steps to convert:

  1. Respond professionally
  2. Reach out privately via phone (better than email—more personal)
  3. Actually listen to their complaint without defending
  4. Offer a genuine resolution (redo treatment, refund, discount on future care, etc.)
  5. Follow up to ensure they're satisfied

When a patient feels truly heard and has their issue resolved, they often become your most loyal advocates.

Monitoring Your Reviews

Set up alerts: Use Google Alerts or a review monitoring service to be notified of new reviews immediately.

Check weekly: Even if you don't get alerts, review your Google profile, Healthgrades, Yelp, and other platforms weekly for new feedback.

Assign responsibility: Designate one person (usually your office manager or front desk lead) to monitor and flag reviews for the dentist to respond to.

Patterns and Systemic Issues

If you see patterns in negative reviews (e.g., multiple complaints about staff, wait times, or specific procedures), treat them as red flags for your practice:

  • Multiple complaints about a staff member? Address it directly.
  • Multiple complaints about long waits? Review your scheduling system.
  • Multiple complaints about a specific procedure? Assess your technique and patient communication.

Negative reviews are feedback. Use them to improve your practice, not just to manage your reputation.

The Bottom Line

Every negative review is an opportunity to show your character. Respond professionally, with empathy, and with a genuine desire to resolve the issue. Most prospective patients understand that every business gets negative reviews—what they judge you on is how you handle them.

Practices with strong responses to negative reviews actually build MORE trust than practices with all positive reviews, because people see they handle adversity with grace and professionalism.
